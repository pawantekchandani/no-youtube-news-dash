## 1. What This System Does

This is a personal news intelligence dashboard that runs on your MacBook. It automatically collects news and analysis from authoritative sources across 12 topic areas, removes duplicates, assigns each article to exactly one topic, summarises each article into two sentences using AI, and presents everything on a single web page you open in your browser at `http://localhost:8000`.

The goal is to replace the habit of opening YouTube repeatedly throughout the day to stay informed. Instead of watching 20 videos on geopolitics, AI, and tech, you open one page in the morning and one in the evening — and everything important is already there, summarised, sourced, and organised.

**The system solves three specific problems:**

1. **Volume** — There are too many sources to follow individually. The system watches all of them for you.

2. **Duplication** — The same news story gets reported by Reuters, Bloomberg, The Hindu, and The Print. The system shows it once, attributed to the highest-authority source.

3. **Depth vs Time** — You get the headline and a 2-sentence summary from a credible source. If something is worth a deeper read, the source link is right there. You choose what to go deeper on.

## 2. Who Uses It and How

**Single user. You.**

The system runs locally on your MacBook. There is no login, no account, no cloud service. It is entirely private.

**Typical daily usage pattern:**

- **8:00 AM** — An email digest arrives in your inbox. Headline + 1-sentence summary for the top 5 articles per topic. Takes 5 minutes to scan.
- **Morning** — If something in the digest catches your interest, open `http://localhost:8000` for the full dashboard with all articles and full 2-sentence summaries.
- **Throughout the day** — The dashboard auto-refreshes every 30 minutes. New articles appear without any action on your part.
- **8:00 PM** — Second email digest arrives with the day's later developments.

**You never need to:**
- Open a terminal once the system is set up
- Manually run any script
- Log into any news website
- Manage any database

## 3. The 12 Topic Areas

The 12 topics are organised into four dashboard columns.

### Column 1 — Geopolitics

**Topic 1: Global Geopolitics**
Major world events — wars, sanctions, diplomatic shifts, treaty negotiations, G20/UN/NATO developments, US-China relations, Russia-Ukraine, Taiwan Strait. Sources include Foreign Affairs, Foreign Policy, Reuters, Bloomberg, The Economist, CSIS, Geopolitical Futures.

**Topic 2: Geopolitics — India Impact**
The same geopolitical world but filtered through the India lens. How do global events affect India's foreign policy, borders, trade, and regional standing? Sources include Observer Research Foundation (ORF), Carnegie India, Takshashila Institution, The Hindu, The Print, Indian Express.

**Topic 3: Sectors to Watch — Tailwinds** *(AI-derived, Phase 3)*
This topic does not have its own news sources. It is generated by AI once per day. The system takes the top geopolitics stories from topics 1 and 2, sends them to Claude, and asks: which market sectors are likely to benefit from today's geopolitical developments? The output is a structured list: sector name, reason, and the specific geopolitical event that drives the inference.

**Topic 4: Sectors Under Pressure** *(AI-derived, Phase 3)*
Same as Topic 3 but for sectors facing headwinds. Same AI process, opposite direction.

### Column 2 — Artificial Intelligence

**Topic 5: AI Space News**
Frontier research, model releases, lab announcements, technical breakthroughs. Sources include MIT Technology Review, arXiv, Import AI (Jack Clark), The Batch (Andrew Ng), Anthropic/OpenAI/DeepMind/Meta AI blogs, Nature Machine Intelligence.

**Topic 6: AI Impact on Business**
How companies are adopting AI, enterprise ROI studies, organisational restructuring, AI strategy. Sources include Harvard Business Review, McKinsey, BCG, Gartner, AI Business.

**Topic 7: AI & Job Market**
Employment effects of AI, displacement research, future of work reports, reskilling. Sources include Stanford HAI, World Economic Forum, Brookings Institution, Wired.

**Topic 8: Unconventional AI Implementations** *(Partially AI-derived, Phase 3)*
Novel, non-obvious applications of AI in hard domains — protein folding, climate modelling, medical diagnosis, materials science. Sources include IEEE Spectrum, Google DeepMind Blog, JMLR, Towards Data Science. In Phase 3, an AI second-pass classifier scores AI news articles for novelty and flags the most unconventional ones here.

### Column 3 — Technology & Markets

**Topic 9: IT Industry News**
Enterprise tech, infrastructure, cybersecurity, hardware, cloud. Sources include Ars Technica, IEEE Spectrum, The Register, ZDNet.

**Topic 10: Big Launches & Updates**
Major software releases, new products, platform updates, engineering announcements. Sources include InfoQ, O'Reilly Radar, The New Stack, Hacker News (high-score posts), Product Hunt.

**Topic 11: Trending on GitHub**
Repositories gaining the most stars this week. Filtered by language (Python, JavaScript, TypeScript, Rust, Go) and minimum star threshold. Sourced by scraping GitHub Trending directly.

**Topic 12: Funding, M&A & Investments**
Venture rounds, acquisitions, mergers, IPOs — globally and specifically India. Sources include Crunchbase News, TechCrunch Venture, PitchBook, Inc42, YourStory, Entrackr.

## 4. Where Content Comes From

### RSS Feeds (Primary Source)
Most sources publish RSS or Atom feeds — machine-readable versions of their latest articles. The system subscribes to these feeds and automatically checks them every 60 minutes. This is the same mechanism used by RSS readers like Feedly, but the content goes into your private database instead of a third-party server.

### GitHub Trending (Scraper)
GitHub does not publish an RSS feed for trending repositories. The system directly reads the GitHub Trending web page and extracts repository names, descriptions, star counts, and languages. This runs on the same 60-minute cycle as the RSS fetcher.

### AI-Generated Insights (Derived)
Topics 3, 4, and 8 are not fetched from any external source. They are produced once per day by sending selected articles from upstream topics to the Claude API and asking structured analysis questions. This runs at 6:00 AM daily.

### What Is Explicitly NOT Used
- YouTube
- Twitter/X
- Reddit
- Any source requiring login
- Any paywalled full-text (paywalled sources contribute their freely-available headlines and abstracts only)

## 5. How Articles Are Processed

Every article goes through a fixed pipeline before appearing on the dashboard.

### Step 1 — Fetch
The system fetches all enabled RSS feeds every 60 minutes. Each new item is stored in a staging area with its URL, title, description, and source.

### Step 2 — Deduplicate
The same story is often reported by multiple sources. Deduplication happens in two stages:

- **Exact match** — If the same URL has been seen before, discard it.
- **Near-match** — If two different URLs have very similar titles (e.g., "Reuters: India imposes sanctions on China" and "Bloomberg: India announces China sanctions"), keep only one. The system keeps the version from the higher-authority source (Tier 1 over Tier 2).

### Step 3 — Classify
Each article is assigned to exactly one topic. The classifier works through the topic list in a fixed priority order and stops at the first match. Topics with more specific signals (India keywords, funding keywords) are checked before broader ones (general AI, general IT).

An article can only appear in one section of the dashboard.

### Step 4 — Summarise *(Phase 2+)*
Each article's title and description are sent to Claude Haiku, which returns a 2-sentence plain-English summary. The summary is stored permanently. If the same article is fetched again (e.g., a source re-publishes it), the cached summary is used — the API is not called again.

In Phase 1, articles appear with the first 200 characters of their original description instead of an AI summary.

### Step 5 — Store and Display
The processed article is written to the database and immediately available on the dashboard at the next page load or refresh.

## 6. The Dashboard

### Layout

The dashboard is a single web page at `http://localhost:8000`. It is divided into four columns:

```
┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┬─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
│   GEOPOLITICS   │       AI        │    TECH &       │    MARKETS      │
│                 │                 │    GITHUB       │                 │
│  • Global Geo   │  • AI News      │  • IT News      │  • Sectors Up   │
│  • India Geo    │  • AI Business  │  • Big Launches │  • Sectors Down │
│                 │  • AI Jobs      │  • GitHub       │                 │
│                 │  • Unconventional│  • Funding/M&A  │                 │
└─────────────────┴─────────────────┴─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
```

### What Each Article Card Shows

- **Headline** — Full article title, clickable link to original source
- **Source badge** — Source name + Tier indicator (Tier 1 sources are visually distinguished)
- **Time** — How long ago the article was published ("2h ago", "Yesterday")
- **Summary** — 2-sentence AI summary (Phase 2+) or first 200 chars of description (Phase 1)

### Sorting Within Each Topic

Articles within each topic section are sorted by published date, newest first.

### Auto-Refresh

The page automatically refreshes every 30 minutes. You do not need to manually reload it. When new articles arrive between refreshes, they appear at the next refresh.

### Manual Refresh

A "Refresh Now" button on the dashboard triggers an immediate pipeline run outside the scheduled cycle. Useful if you want the very latest news on demand.

## 7. The Email Digest

Two emails are sent daily: 8:00 AM and 8:00 PM.

### What the Email Contains

Each email covers all 12 topic sections. For each topic, the top 5 most recent articles are listed with:
- Article headline
- 1-sentence summary (shortened from the 2-sentence dashboard version)
- Source name
- Link to original article

### Format

The email is sent in both plain text and HTML. The HTML version has minimal formatting — clean and readable on any email client including mobile.

### How It Is Sent

The email is sent from your MacBook using your local mail configuration (macOS Mail SMTP). If you prefer, you can configure it to use Resend (free tier: 100 emails/day) or any SMTP server you specify in the configuration.

### Who Receives It

The recipient email address is set in `sources.yaml`. Default is your own email address. This is a private digest — it goes only to you.

## 9. Configuration — What You Can Change Without Code

Everything in this section is controlled by editing `config/sources.yaml` in a text editor. No Python knowledge required.

### Add a New RSS Feed

Add a new entry under `feeds:` with the feed's URL, name, and which topic it belongs to. The feed will be picked up on the next pipeline run.

### Disable a Feed Temporarily

Change `enabled: true` to `enabled: false`. The feed is ignored until you re-enable it. Useful when a source goes behind a paywall or starts publishing low-quality content.

### Change How Often Feeds Are Fetched

Edit `pipeline.fetch_interval_minutes`. Default is 60. Set to 30 for more frequent updates, or 120 to reduce API load.

### Change the Digest Schedule

Edit `digest.schedule` to add, remove, or change the times of day when email digests are sent.

### Change How Many Articles Appear in the Digest

Edit `digest.top_n_per_topic`. Default is 5. Set to 10 if you want a more detailed digest.

### Change How Old Articles Can Be Before They Are Hidden

Edit `pipeline.dashboard_article_age_hours`. Default is 24 hours. Set to 48 if you want two days of history on the dashboard.

### Add Keywords to a Topic

Each topic in `sources.yaml` has a `keywords` list. Add any word or phrase to help the classifier correctly assign articles to that topic.

### Change the Deduplication Sensitivity

Edit `pipeline.dedupe_similarity_threshold`. Default is 85 (out of 100). Increase to 90 if you are seeing too many similar articles; decrease to 75 if unique articles are being incorrectly merged.

## 10. What This System Does NOT Do

Being explicit about boundaries prevents confusion.

| What You Might Expect | What Actually Happens |
|----------------------|----------------------|
| Full article text | Headline + description/abstract only. Click the link for full text. |
| Video summaries | No video content. Text only. |
| Paywalled full text | Paywalled sources contribute only what is freely available in their RSS feed (usually headline + abstract). |
| Personalisation / recommendations | No "you might also like." Content is curated by source selection, not by your reading history. |
| Real-time alerts | Updates every 60 minutes, not instantly. Not a news alert system. |
| Social media | No Twitter, Reddit, LinkedIn, or YouTube. |
| Email newsletters without RSS | Newsletters that do not publish an RSS feed are excluded (e.g. some Substack writers who disable RSS). |
| Mobile app | Browser only. Opens in any browser on your MacBook. Not optimised for phone. |
| Search within articles | Not available in Phase 1–4. Planned for Phase 5. |

## 11. Phase-by-Phase Feature Availability

### Phase 1 — Working Aggregator (No AI)
- All RSS feeds fetched every 60 minutes
- GitHub Trending scraped
- Deduplication active
- Keyword-based topic classification
- Dashboard available at `http://localhost:8000`
- Articles show title + raw description (first 200 chars)
- No summaries
- No email digest yet
- No derived topics (3, 4, 8)

**This is a complete, useful product. It already removes the need to check 40+ sources manually.**

### Phase 2 — AI Summaries
Everything from Phase 1, plus:
- Every article has a 2-sentence AI-generated summary
- Summaries are cached — same article never summarised twice

### Phase 3 — Derived Topics
Everything from Phase 2, plus:
- Topic 3 (Sectors to Watch) populated daily
- Topic 4 (Sectors Under Pressure) populated daily
- Topic 8 (Unconventional AI) populated daily

### Phase 4 — Automation & Digest
Everything from Phase 3, plus:
- macOS launchd service — server starts on login, no terminal needed
- Email digest at 8:00 AM and 8:00 PM daily
- Automatic article cleanup

### Phase 5 — Quality Layer
Everything from Phase 4, plus:
- Embedding-based deduplication (catches paraphrased duplicates that fool keyword dedup)
- Authority scoring (Tier 1 articles ranked above Tier 2 within same topic)
- Mark as read (articles you have read are greyed out)
- Reading time estimate on each card

## 12. Edge Cases and Behaviour Rules

### A feed is temporarily down
The pipeline continues with all other feeds. The failed feed is logged. On the next run (60 minutes later), it is retried. A single feed failure never stops the full pipeline.

### The Claude API is unavailable
Articles are stored and classified as normal but without summaries. The dashboard shows the raw description snippet instead. Summarisation is retried on the next pipeline run when the API is available again. The system never crashes or blocks due to LLM unavailability.

### An article matches two topic keywords equally
The priority order in `sources.yaml` resolves the tie. The first matching topic in the priority list wins. The priority order can be edited in configuration if you disagree with any default assignment.

### A Tier 2 article is kept over a Tier 1 article during dedup
This can happen if the Tier 1 source was fetched later (i.e., the Tier 2 version was stored first). In Phase 5, authority scoring will handle this more cleanly. In Phase 1–4, the first-seen version is kept if the URL is exactly the same; for near-duplicate titles, Tier 1 wins.

### The GitHub Trending page changes structure
The GitHub scraper will break silently. The `github_trending` section will show no new items. Set `github.enabled: false` in `sources.yaml` to disable until the scraper is fixed. This does not affect any other topic.

### An article from a geopolitics source talks about AI
The classifier checks `geopolitics_india` first, then `geopolitics`, then `it_funding`, and so on. If the article matches geopolitics keywords, it is classified as geopolitics — even if it also mentions AI. The topic priority order determines outcome. To change this behaviour, edit the priority values in `sources.yaml`.

### The email digest fails to send
The digest failure is logged. The dashboard is unaffected. The next scheduled digest attempt will proceed normally. No retry for the failed digest — missed digests are not queued.

### You add a new feed that publishes 500 articles at once
The deduplication and classification pipeline handles the volume. However, the LLM summarisation step (Phase 2) will batch these across multiple API calls. Expect a slightly longer pipeline run time for the first fetch of a new high-volume feed. Subsequent runs will only process new items.

### An article is published with a future timestamp (common bug in some RSS feeds)
Articles with a published_at timestamp more than 2 hours in the future are treated as if published at the time of fetching. They appear at the top of their topic section and will naturally sort downward as newer articles arrive.
